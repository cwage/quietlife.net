I wrote a good old fashioned crazy old lady letter to [GQ][1] tonight, in response to an article by this guy extolling the "virtues" of his apparent insolent lack of concern for his health or diet. Your typical "so what if I weigh 250 pounds and I'm gonna die -- if I act insolent about my complete lack of self-control it seems like I do it in purpose even though I hate myself and cry on the inside every time I wolf down a big mac" spiel.

It's a little pointless without the context of the article, so if you want, read it in the May 2002 issue of GQ -- it's in the "American Scene" section.

Here it is, anyway:

<div class="quote">
  Subject: regarding Rick Bragg's "American Scene" -- For a vegetable, I'll Have White Gravy<br /> To: gqmag@aol.com<br /> Date: Mon, 22 Apr 2002 23:26:34 -0500</p> 
  
  <p>
    As a southern man, I am disturbed that Rick Bragg paints a portrait of<br /> the ideal southern man as a hedonistic, self-indulgent gourmand. I<br /> grew up in the south, and I enjoy and appreciate a good meat-n-three<br /> as I do any other cuisine. However, I also plan on living past 50.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    Southern cuisine was born of a time when southern Americans worked --<br /> and worked hard. While I'm sure Mr. Bragg works hard, too, I doubt<br /> that he's out there splittin' rails. And neither am I. I work in the<br /> computer industry, and it takes a certain amount of effort and<br /> moderation to maintain physical fitness and health.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    Obesity is fast-approaching smoking as the leading cause of<br /> preventable death in the United States. Mr. Bragg is setting a poor<br /> example, and is a sad representative of a "southern man". If he wants<br /> to eat himself into a coronary, that's fine, but it's nothing to be<br /> proud of.
  </p>
  
  <p>
    --Chris Wage<br /> cwage@place.org<br /> http://chris.agenteight.com/ </div>
