Posting a recipe for marinara is admittedly silly, since few things are probably as well-documented, and marinara is not exactly brain surgery. But, for the sake of documenting what I did, here it is. What good is the Internet if we can't endlessly analyze and document the most trivial of processes? Also, pictures.

  1. Ingredient 1: a bunch of <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8284/7641545896_466b5b0af2_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00059 by cwage, on Flickr">heirloom tomatoes</a> from the farmer's market, peeled and (mostly) seeded.
  2. I used the method for peeling and seeding where you score the tomatoes, blanche for 15-20 seconds and then chill in ice water bath. Peel them, and then put the tomatoes in a colander over a bowl (to save the precious, precious juice) and use your fingers to pierce/seed the tomatoes. Strain the seeds, saving as much of the juice and meat as you can, toss the seeds, <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8289/7641540718_3c526723df_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00054 by cwage, on Flickr">combine with the tomatoes</a>. Don't sweat getting every little seed, but try to get the bulk of them, as they can add bitterness and they get stuck in your teeth.
  3. Ingredients 2 and 3: <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8430/7641536498_ec7622640e_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00051 by cwage, on Flickr">garlic and onions</a>.
  4. Ingredient 3: <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7263/7641559930_c432993952_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00069 by cwage, on Flickr">basil</a>.
  5. <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8153/7651575752_aafe1481c4_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00062 by cwage, on Flickr">Sauté garlic and onions</a> in olive oil and high heat until soft/translucent.
  6. <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8005/7651586252_e79d61f8d8_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00065 by cwage, on Flickr">Cool/deglaze with white wine and/or water</a>. I used some leftover sauvignon blanc, but typically I'd pick something less sweet. Add tomatoes/juice. <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7134/7641553156_4dce53c5f4_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00067 by cwage, on Flickr">Add water to cover</a> as necessary. 
      * <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7140/7641575592_99ce2d1bd4_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00084 by cwage, on Flickr">Season with basil</a> and oregano (I had no fresh oregano, so I used dried.). Oooooor, do this at the end, closer to serving time. See notes below.
      * Bring it all back to near a slow boil (I never let it get too hot because I was paranoid about burning the basil/bitterness). <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm9.staticflickr.com/8147/7641582506_9798b01fca_b.jpg" title="2012-07-24-00085 by cwage, on Flickr">Simmer on low</a>. I let this simmer for around 20 hours, covered, and then removed the cover to start boiling off water content.
      * <a rel="lyteshow[frontpage]" href="http://farm8.staticflickr.com/7117/7651638960_a93d337641_b.jpg" title="2012-07-25-00106 by cwage, on Flickr">The result</a> after around 24 hours.</ol> 
    Notes:
    
      * The heirloom tomatoes I got had the "green shoulders" or whatever the term is -- a much more fibrous/green stem area at the top. Rather than meticulously trimming it away, I just dumped the tomatoes in whole and worried about the stems after it cooked down a bit (fished out each top, trimmed away most of the meat, and tossed the stem). Tedious, but not a big deal. Worth it for the flavor of these tomatoes, in my opinion.
      * Only after I got everything stewing did it occur to me that I've heard that basil can get bitter if you cook it too long. I rolled with it anyway. After around 24 hours of total cooking, I didn't notice any bitterness imparted. Possible theories: I don't notice or don't mind whatever bitterness it imparted? Maybe basil only gets bitter when cooked too *hot*, and not necessarily long? Maybe the whole thing is bunk. I don't know. But it seemed fine. Add the basil at the end if you're worried about this.
      * It was pretty damn good. Anyone have any suggestions for improvement?
